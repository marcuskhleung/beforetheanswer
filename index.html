The problem

What AI dependency is doing to young minds

The concern isn't AI itself — it's the way passive use is replacing the mental effort that builds real cognitive capacity.

Memory and recall erosion

When students look up every answer instantly, the retrieval practice that cements long-term memory never happens. Cognitive load is reduced so much that learning itself is bypassed.

Memory

Reduced problem-solving tolerance

Sitting with a hard question builds resilience. AI removes that discomfort entirely — students are losing the capacity to persist when answers don't come immediately.

Resilience

Declining original thought

Writing and argumentation require forming an original idea. When AI scaffolds every draft, students practice assembling — not thinking. The inner voice weakens from disuse.

Creativity

Social and emotional reasoning gaps

Reading tone, navigating disagreement, and interpreting ambiguity are learned through friction. AI mediates too much of that friction, stunting social cognition.

Social skills

Atrophied critical evaluation

When a confident-sounding AI answer ends inquiry, students never develop the skeptical habits that distinguish analysis from acceptance of information at face value.

Critical thinking

Shortened attention spans

Deep focus — the ability to hold a complex idea and work through it — is built over time. AI-accelerated workflows actively train students away from sustained concentration.

For educators

Activities that build brains back

Practical, classroom-ready strategies that restore the productive struggle AI has been quietly removing from students' lives.

01Slow paper thinking

Give students a complex question and 8 minutes of silent, pen-only thinking time before any discussion. No devices. No collaboration. The discomfort is the point.

All grades10 min
02Brain dump retrieval

Start every class with a blank page. Students write everything they remember from the previous lesson — no notes, no prompts. Score it together. Builds retrieval strength fast.

Grades 4–125–8 min
03Structured controversy

Assign students a position they didn't choose on a real issue. They must argue it convincingly, then switch and argue the opposing side. Forces genuine reasoning over reflex.

Grades 6–1225–40 min
04Constraint creation

Assign a creative task with a hard constraint: a story with no adjectives, a solution using only 3 objects, a drawing with only straight lines. Constraints force original thinking.

All grades20–30 min
05Steel-man practice

Present students with a weak or clearly flawed argument. Their job is to make the strongest possible version of it. Builds intellectual empathy and genuine analytical depth.

Grades 7–1215–20 min
06Teach-back in pairs

After a lesson, students pair up and take turns teaching it to each other from scratch — no notes. The act of explaining without resources forces genuine encoding.

All grades10–15 min
07Socratic seminar

Teacher asks only questions — never answers. Students must build understanding together through dialogue, challenge each other's reasoning, and sit with genuine uncertainty.

Grades 5–1230–45 min
08Hypothetical worlds

Pose "what if" scenarios rooted in the curriculum. Students must reason within the constraints, building inferential thinking and imaginative logic.

All grades20–30 min
09Error hunt

Give students an AI-generated response with deliberate errors, logical gaps, and unsupported claims. Their job: find everything wrong with it.

Grades 5–1215–20 min
