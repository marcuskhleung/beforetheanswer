Critical thinking & cognitive offloading

More Students Use AI for Homework, and More Believe It Harms Critical Thinking
Schwartz, H.L. & Diliberti, M.K. — RAND Corporation (March 2026)
As of December 2025, 67% of students endorsed the statement "The more students use AI for their schoolwork, the more it will harm their critical thinking skills" — up more than 10 percentage points from ten months earlier. Survey of 1,214 American youth.

Rising Use of AI in Schools Comes With Big Downsides for Students
Center for Democracy and Technology (2025)
70% of teachers worry that AI weakens critical thinking and research skills. 85% of teachers and 86% of students used AI in the 2024–25 school year. Half of students agree that using AI in class makes them feel less connected to their teachers.
Critical thinking Teacher survey Read the article →
Evaluating the Impact of AI on Critical Thinking Skills Among Higher Education Students
Frontiers in Education (2025)
Students who rely heavily on AI demonstrate substantial declines in analytical reasoning capabilities. Overuse of AI creates "cognitive offloading" — students become less able to analyse information independently, formulate logical arguments, and make reasoned decisions. Younger students are particularly susceptible.
Peer-reviewed Cognitive offloading Read the study →
How Gen Z Uses Gen AI — and Why It Worries Them
Harvard Business Review (January 2026)
MIT Media Lab study of 54 undergraduates found that writing with AI induced "cognitive debt" — EEG scans showed decreased brain activity, and most students who used AI were later unable to recall a sentence from what they'd written. 79% of young adults expressed concern that AI makes people lazier; 62% worried it makes people less smart.

Attention spans & focus

Attention Span Research — Digital Screen Focus Duration
Dr. Gloria Mark, University of California Irvine (2023)
The average attention span on a digital device was approximately 150 seconds in 2004. By 2012 it had dropped to 75 seconds. By 2024, research estimates it has reached a low of approximately 47 seconds — a 68% decline over two decades.

Social Media Use Linked to Declining Focus and Emotional Strain in Youth
Nanyang Technological University Singapore & Research Network (July 2025)
Survey of 583 young people aged 13–25 across Singapore and Australia found that 68% reported difficulty focusing, with many struggling to complete schoolwork or engage with content lasting more than a minute. Prolonged social media use associated with emotional fatigue and compulsive behaviors.

"Screen Time" to "AI Time": Cognitive, Emotional, and Behavioral Outcomes in Children and Adolescents
PMC / National Institutes of Health (2024)
Peer-reviewed analysis calling for urgent adaptation of public health surveillance to address how AI use — not just screen time — is undermining the cognitive, emotional, and social development of today's youth.

Teacher perspectives & classroom impact

Survey: 60% of Teachers Used AI This Year
Gallup / The 74 (July 2025)
57% of teachers said weekly AI use by students would decrease their independent thinking. 52% said it would decrease critical thinking. Nearly half said it would decrease student persistence in solving problems, ability to build meaningful relationships, and resilience.

Over Half of Teachers Say AI Is Harming Students' Critical Thinking
NPR / Ipsos Poll — K-12 Dive (2026)
NPR/Ipsos survey of 545 K-12 teachers found that over half believe AI is actively harming students' ability to think critically — one of the most direct teacher assessments on record.

Gen Z's own concerns about AI

How Gen Z Is Using AI
Harvard Business School Publishing (February 2026)
79% of young adults expressed concern that AI makes people lazier. 62% worried it makes them less smart. Gen Z specifically worries that AI will displace three important practices: learning by doing, thinking critically, and learning from other people.
Gen Z survey Harvard Read the article →
Gen Z's AI Adoption Steady, but Skepticism Climbs
Gallup (April 2026)
8 in 10 Gen Zers say it is "very" or "somewhat" likely that using AI tools will make it more difficult for them to learn in the future. Gen Z is not convinced that AI enhances creativity or critical thinking, and the majority believe it may come at a cost to learning.
Gen Z survey Gallup Read the report →
Gen Z Is Using AI — But Reports Gaps in School and Workplace Support
Walton Family Foundation / Gallup (April 2025)
Nearly half (49%) of Gen Z are concerned that AI will harm their ability to think carefully about information. 47% use generative AI weekly. 41% feel anxious about the technology.

About our research standards

We prioritise peer-reviewed studies, major institutional surveys (Gallup, RAND, Harvard, NIH), and direct reporting from established education publications. Where research is emerging or contested, we aim to represent that complexity rather than overstate certainty. This page is updated as new research becomes available. Last updated: June 2026.
